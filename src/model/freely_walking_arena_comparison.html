Arena Geometries & When Centripetal Drift Can Fail

How These Six Arenas Differ

Circular (∅ 241 mm)

Smooth, isotropic boundary. Strong centripetal drift expected — the canonical case.

Square (241 mm side)

Flat walls, right-angle corners. Diagonal viewing distances are √2 longer than axial, so the drift is slightly weaker along the diagonals. Net result: a mildly diamond-shaped distribution.

Oval (420 mm × 120 mm, 3.5:1)

Strongly asymmetric viewing distances: ~210 mm along the major axis (very low gain) vs ~60 mm along the minor (high gain). The minor-axis walls drive strong lateral centring, but the gain along the long axis is so weak that flies become quasi-1D random walkers and accumulate near the tips of the oval rather than at the centre.

Curved Cross (arm: 280 mm × 110 mm)

Two oval arms unioned. Each arm has long axial viewing distance (low gain → drift toward tip) and short lateral distance (high gain → centring within the arm). With high k_dist, flies cluster at the four arm tips, not the geometric centre.

Donut (R_out 120 mm, R_in 45 mm)

Annular ring with a central obstacle. Both walls (inner and outer) generate the same wall-repulsion mechanism. The two repulsions balance at the midpoint of the annulus, so flies should cluster in a ring at radius ~83 mm — the new "centre". The geometric centre is unreachable.

Crescent (concave cutout)

Outer circle (R 120 mm) minus an offset cutout (R 65 mm at +80 mm x). The cutout boundary is concave from inside. A fly in the concave pocket sees the cutout wall close on one side and the outer wall close on the other — it gets squeezed between two walls. Watch whether flies cluster on the convex (left) side, away from the pocket.

Geometries Where Centripetal Drift Can Break Down

The drift mechanism relies on a consistent asymmetry: facing the wall → short viewing distance → high gain → strong turning; facing the interior → long viewing distance → low gain → straighter path. This asymmetry is broken when:

  • The "centre" is geometrically inaccessible (donut): Flies cannot drift to a point; they drift to whatever locus minimises the average wall-proximity gain. For the annulus this is the midpoint ring.
  • The arena has multiple competing attractor regions (cross, star): Each arm tip becomes a local attractor because the gain landscape favours moving outward along the arm. The geometric centre becomes a saddle rather than an attractor.
  • The boundary is concave (crescent, hourglass, horseshoe): In the concave region, the wall-normal points away from where you'd want flies to drift. The pocket can trap flies or push them in unexpected directions, depending on whether the convex outer wall or concave inner wall dominates locally.
  • The arena is highly elongated (oval > 3:1): The major-axis viewing distance gets so long that the gain is effectively zero there. Flies become quasi-1D random walkers along the major axis with reflective endpoints, accumulating at the tips. The 3.5:1 oval in this page is in this regime — at moderate-to-high k_dist, the tip clustering should be clearly visible.

Model Equations (identical in all arenas)

Δθ = grating_dir × grating_speed × dt  +  randn()/1.2 × √dt  +  grating_dir × k_dist × sigmoid(viewing_dist) × dt
sigmoid(d) = 1 / (1 + exp(0.02 × (d − 90)))      speed = v_max / (1 + 7 × |Δθ|)

The only geometry-dependent quantity is viewing_dist — the distance from the fly's current position to the nearest wall along its heading. Each arena uses a different ray-intersection formula. Boundary reflections use the wall's outward normal (angle-of-incidence = angle-of-reflection), except the simple circular arena which uses a 180° flip.
