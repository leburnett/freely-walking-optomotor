The Two-Eye Model: Bilateral Optic-Flow Comparison

Biological Motivation

In a real fly, each compound eye independently samples the visual world. When the gratings on the arena wall move, the perceived angular velocity on each retina depends on the geometry of viewing — specifically, how far the wall is from each eye. A wall that is close fills more of the visual field and produces faster apparent motion than a wall far away, even if both walls carry identical gratings moving at identical speeds.

This model replaces the original single-viewing-distance gain with two independent eye signals, then drives turning based on a comparison (here: the average) of those two signals.

Eye Geometry

Each fly carries two eyes at the front of its (oval) body, one on either side. The representative line of sight for each eye points ±45° from the body axis — forward-and-laterally — approximating where the foveal axis of Drosophila compound eyes is directed. For each eye, the model casts a ray from the body centre in the eye's viewing direction and computes the distance to the arena wall (d_left, d_right).

Per-Eye Angular Velocity (Raw)

The wall surface moves at linear speed v_wall = grating_speed × R_arena (tangentially, since the gratings rotate around the arena). When viewed from distance d, this produces an apparent retinal angular velocity inversely proportional to distance:

ω_raw = grating_speed × R_arena / d_eye

Reichardt-Like EMD Saturation

Real elementary motion detectors (EMDs) in the fly visual system have a non-monotonic velocity tuning: response grows with stimulus speed up to a preferred velocity ω_pref, then decreases at higher velocities (an aliasing-driven dropoff). We approximate this with a saturating function:

ω_eye = ω_raw / (1 + (ω_raw / ω_pref)2)

This response is approximately linear at low velocities, peaks at ω_raw = ω_pref with a value of ω_pref / 2, then decreases. Typical Drosophila EMD preferred velocities are in the range 1–5 rad/s. Both eyes apply this nonlinearity independently.

Turning Rule: Baseline + Bilateral Excess

The turning command splits into two parts: a uniform optomotor baseline that is always present whenever the gratings move, plus a bilateral wall-proximity amplification scaled by k_turn:

ω_avg = (ω_left + ω_right) / 2
ω_baseline = Reichardt(grating_speed, ω_pref) // what each eye sees at the centre
ω_excess = ω_avg − ω_baseline
dθ/dt = grating_dir × (ω_baseline + k_turn × ω_excess)  +  brownian

With k_turn = 0, every fly turns at the baseline rate regardless of position (uniform optomotor response). With k_turn > 0, the wall-proximity excess on top of that baseline increases turning whenever a fly's eyes see close walls. Always has the sign of the grating motion, as you specified. Speed-turning coupling is preserved: speed = v_max / (1 + 7 × |dθ|).

Comparison With the Original Single-View Model

Original (single forward view)

Turning gain depends on where the fly is heading. Heading toward a wall → short forward viewing distance → high gain → tight turning. Heading toward the centre → long view → low gain → straight path. Centripetal drift emerges directly from this heading-dependent asymmetry.

Two-eye (this model)

Turning gain depends on where the fly is, integrated over two lateral views. Near the wall, at least one eye almost always sees a short distance, so turning is faster. The forward viewing distance no longer enters explicitly. Centring (if it emerges) comes from position-dependent turning rate rather than heading-dependent gain.

Will Centring Emerge?

The mechanism is more indirect than in the single-view model. Tighter turning near the wall means smaller turn radii there — a fly near the wall traces small circles, while a fly near the centre traces large ones. Combined with Brownian noise, this should produce a statistical bias toward the centre, but it depends on the geometry of the eye signals. The centring may be weaker, may emerge only at certain k_turn values, or may show qualitatively different distributions (e.g., a ring rather than a single peak). Watch for it!
